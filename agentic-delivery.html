Agentic Delivery Architecture

The squad is getting smaller. The system around it cannot remain manual.

Traditional delivery divided work across a stack of specialists. Agile brought those specialists into a two-pizza team. Agentic delivery compresses the team again: a domain authority and a Forward Deployed Engineer, supported by agents and a shared enterprise delivery system.

The challenge is no longer how to staff a squad. It is how to engineer the architecture, controls, support and organisational memory that let a two-person cell own an outcome safely.

The squad evolution

From a stack of specialists, to a persistent team, to a smaller team using agents, to a domain authority and a Forward Deployed Engineer each orchestrating specialist agents. Five people are one point on an evolving spectrum, not the final canonical shape.

{% include diagrams/squad-evolution.html %}

The 1:1 model will not suit every production service, but it becomes the default starting shape for bounded workflows and Domain Building Blocks. Extra people are pulled in because the work needs them, not because a seat must be filled.

The cell shrinks because the organisation carries more

A two-person cell is not a smaller version of a squad. It is a squad that has moved most of its context, constraints and controls out of people's heads and into the organisation around it. The shrinking squad is a projection of the whole model, not a workforce trend on its own. The 1:1 cell works only when five things can be supplied on demand, not rediscovered per project.

Trusted context

The cell inherits the reasoning behind the system it is changing, not just the code. Every material decision is recorded as a Transformation Decision Record: executable decision context an agent and a human can both act on, so the cell knows why the system is the way it is. See TDRs and the ledger →

Reusable knowledge

It does not start from a blank page. Knowledge Intelligence and the decision graph connect decisions, capabilities, evidence and outcomes, so domain knowledge is drawn on rather than reconstructed each time a cell forms.

Constraints

Autonomy needs boundaries that travel with the work. Each agent runs under a machine-readable Agent Contract: mandate, permissions, operating boundary and revocation, with the actor verified by Know Your Agent before it acts.

Evidence

Decisions, outcomes and model versions are written to the shared trust ledger, so what the cell did, and under what authority, is provable after the fact rather than argued in a meeting.

Escalation

When a decision is novel or falls outside the boundary, the cell does not improvise. Architecture, risk, legal and compliance are on call through the capability mesh, and exceptions route to an accountable human.

Take any one away

and the cell stops being accountable autonomy and becomes unmanaged risk. This is why the model is a system design question first, and a staffing question second.

The system around the 1:1 agentic cell

Those five needs are engineered, not hoped for. The cell sits inside a paved road, wrapped by an accountability backbone. This is not AI-amplified delivery. It is a unit of accountable autonomy.

{% include diagrams/agentic-cell-system.html %}

1. Agentic paved road

A pre-integrated route from idea to production: approved models, tools and connectors; reusable agent skills and Domain Building Blocks; isolated environments; automated testing, evaluation and policy checks; deployment, observability and cost metering; versioned prompts, models, data, code and agent configurations. Without it, every cell rebuilds a fragile local stack.

2. Trust and control plane

Controls travel with the work, not through meetings: agent identity and Know Your Agent; machine-readable Agent Contracts; mandates, permissions and operating boundaries; TDRs for material decisions; evidence, outcomes and model versions written to the trust ledger; revocation, rollback and kill switches.

3. Production support

A two-person cell cannot recreate a bank's operational machinery. It draws on shared service ownership and on-call; incident, problem and change pathways designed for agents; resilience and recovery testing; agent failure and degradation patterns; human takeover and exception handling; common operational telemetry.

4. Enterprise capability mesh

Architecture, data, cyber, risk, legal, compliance and procurement move from permanent seats and committee gates into reusable services: controls as code; architecture fitness functions; approved data products; reusable contractual and regulatory patterns; automated assurance; specialists on call for exceptions and novel decisions.

5. Workforce and learning pipeline

A senior 1:1 model must create the next generation, not consume expertise: rotating apprentices and emerging engineers; recorded decisions and reusable agent skills; structured shadowing of domain experts; progression from operator to orchestrator to accountable owner; deliberate transfer of supplier knowledge into the client.

The centre gets smaller, and more capable

It owns the paved road, the reference architecture and the trust ledger. It does not own the squad's backlog.

The work changes shape, not just size

When agents produce most of the code, the scarce human work moves up. The two people in the cell are not doing the old five jobs faster. They are doing different work.

From producing to framing

The domain authority owns the problem and the outcome. The Forward Deployed Engineer frames the work for agents, sets the constraints and checks the evidence. Neither is measured by lines produced.

Judgement and systems thinking

The value sits in the trade-offs an agent cannot own: what good looks like, where the risk really is, how one decision moves the wider system. This is why the model is senior by default.

Governing autonomous execution

The human role becomes directing and assuring agents that act: setting mandates, reading outcomes, intervening when the evidence says so. Governing execution, not performing it.

This is a talent redesign, not a headcount cut. If the first rungs of the engineering ladder were where people learned the craft, and agents now do that work, the ladder has to be rebuilt rather than removed. Emerging engineers learn by orchestrating and reviewing agents, shadowing domain experts and reading the recorded decisions, progressing from operator to orchestrator to accountable owner. Leadership shifts with them: managing mandates and outcomes, not seats and utilisation. The operating model sets out how that apprenticeship is rebuilt.

Beyond the demonstration

Uber has publicly described the forward-deployed pair as a discovery mechanism: an AI-proficient engineer beside a domain expert, observing the real work, redesigning the workflow and shipping in weeks. That validates the shape. It describes the intervention, not yet the complete production operating model.

The unanswered question is what must become a shared organisational capability so that a two-person cell can safely own a production outcome. That is the question this page answers. Uber shows how to discover and automate a workflow. DTA describes how the resulting delivery model becomes governable, supportable and repeatable across a regulated enterprise.

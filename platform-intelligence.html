Execute the change

Platform Intelligence

The question: how are we actually going to execute this change — safely, and at a cost we can see?

Every transformation ultimately succeeds or fails in execution. The other seven Intelligences answer what to change and why. Platform Intelligence answers how: what execution really costs, what the existing estate forces you to inherit, and how the new platform is delivered alongside the old without breaking what customers depend on today. It is the discipline of executing institutional change — evolution over rip-and-replace, made concrete.

The other seven ask what. Platform asks how.

Execution is a sequence, not an event. Platform Intelligence is where every other lens’s decision becomes a delivered, evidenced step — from the business case to the platform you finish with.

Business case→ Existing estate→ Roadmap→ Migration→ Parallel run→ Cutover→ Remediation→ Synergy→ Future platform

Every Intelligence contributes to this line. Value says why; Financial says whether it can be funded; Capability says whether people can deliver it; Experience says whether customers benefit; Control says whether it stays within risk appetite; Cognitive says where AI takes part. Platform Intelligence integrates all of it into delivery.

What execution actually costs

Financial Intelligence asks should we fund this and can we afford the transition. Platform Intelligence shows what execution actually costs — and where it pays back. Almost every programme underestimates the two phases in the middle.

Illustrative. Transformation costs more before it costs less: running both estates, plus remediating the old one, lifts cost above today’s before the lower target run-rate arrives. Synergy realisation — retiring duplicate licences, teams, processes, controls and infrastructure — pulls it lower still. (This is the cost of execution; Financial’s J-curve is the return on it.)

The Transformation Balance Sheet

Before the roadmap, the honest view: what is worth preserving, what is creating drag, what you are investing in, and what it should return. The liabilities — the debt in the existing estate — set the real cost of change far more than the target architecture does.

Assets

Worth preserving: customer trust, institutional knowledge, proven processes, products, licences, skills and data.

Liabilities

Creating drag: technical, data, operational, control and contract debt; vendor lock-in; unsupported software; manual workarounds; weak documentation.

Investments

New capability: AI, tokenisation, cloud, digital identity, payments — and the platform that carries them.

Returns

What it should yield: revenue, lower cost, resilience, better consumer outcomes and future optionality.

Executed safely: coexistence, not rip-and-replace

The existing core was not built for agentic processes or a shared regulated ledger. So the new platform runs in parallel: change data capture keeps an immutable ledger in step, a gateway controls what each channel reaches, and the core stays the system of record for regulatory reporting until every function has moved.

Illustrative. The gateway routes each channel to the new platform where it is ready and to the legacy core where it is not. Change data capture feeds the immutable ledger so the two stay in step. The new ledger is built to take part in a Regulated Liability Network (RLN), settling tokenised regulated liabilities on shared, governed infrastructure. The core stays the system of record for regulatory reporting and is the off-ramp, decommissioned function by function.

Illustrative. The strangler-fig pattern: the new platform grows around the core, wave by wave, and AI accelerates each migration so more moves sooner. Nothing depends on a single big-bang cutover — and the evidence from each proven wave is what convinces the institution the target is less painful than the disease.

Owned, not rented

Where the platform runs is now a board-level resilience question. Concentration risk, a stressed exit from a single provider, and a shifting geopolitical map sit squarely under DORA and the PRA. We build for operational sovereignty: on-device and small language models where data must not leave the building, a stack you can exit, the keys held by the bank, and custody behind a swappable port so no single provider is load-bearing. Rent the platform from a vendor or a protocol and you have rented your resilience with it. Owned Autonomy means the working capability, and the ability to walk away, stay yours. Expressed in open standards (ISO 20022, BIAN) so what it proves is a portable, vendor-neutral working specification rather than a single supplier’s stack.

Capacity to change, and the option to change course

Two things Platform Intelligence protects that programmes routinely lose.

Transformation Capacity

Every institution has a finite ability to absorb change — not just budget, but capacity: technology, operations, people, control, vendors, testing and migration. Sequencing a programme beyond that capacity is how delivery stalls. This is where Platform meets Capability.

Programme optionality

Programmes become irreversible when they should stay optional. We hold the option open: small commitments, evidence, decision gates, reversible architecture, parallel running and incremental funding — transformation as a series of proven decisions, not one irreversible bet.

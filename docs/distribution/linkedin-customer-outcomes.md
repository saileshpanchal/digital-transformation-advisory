## Post A — the main thought-leadership post (acknowledged problem)

The FCA has now told banks the same thing three years running, and it is worth saying plainly:

You can show what you did. You usually cannot show whether it made things better for the customer.

In its 2024 review of outcomes monitoring across twenty larger insurers, the FCA found firms still building their monitoring on repackaged existing data, thresholds set so they were almost impossible to breach, and red-amber-green ratings that stayed green while the underlying trend was falling. Its board-report reviews found the same from the top: process-completion metrics standing in for outcome metrics.

The finding that should stop any transformation leader cold: firms could not demonstrate how improvements to customer outcomes were monitored *after a change was made.*

Think about what that means. A firm finds a poor outcome, changes something to fix it, and then cannot prove the fix worked. That is the entire job of a transformation, and it is the exact part the data could not evidence.

This is not laziness. It is architecture. The outcome the Consumer Duty asks about, did the customer understand it, did it suit them, did the change help, was never written down at the point of action. So every board report becomes a reconstruction project: analysts joining spreadsheets to find vulnerable customers, hand-building narrative before anyone can read the number.

No dashboard fixes a substrate that threw the answer away.

The fix is structural. Treat each change as a hypothesis about customer behaviour. Define the outcome and the metric before you ship, wire the measurement to the change, and record the evidence as the action runs. Then "prove the change worked" stops being the hardest slide in the pack and becomes a query you can run.

## Post B — short hook (for reshare / second slot)

"We shipped the change" is not the same as "the change worked."

The FCA keeps finding the gap: firms that can report activity but cannot evidence whether a fix actually improved the customer's outcome, by group, after the change was made.

That is not a reporting problem you can write your way out of. It is a data problem. The outcome was never recorded at the point of action, so it gets reconstructed by hand, every board cycle.

Build the measurement into the change instead of bolting it on after. The before-and-after exists because you defined it up front.

## Post C — comment-bait / poll framing (optional)

Honest question for anyone doing Consumer Duty board reporting:

When you change something to fix a poor customer outcome, can you show, from your own data, that it worked? By customer group? Without an analyst spending a fortnight rebuilding the picture?

The FCA's reviews suggest most firms still cannot, and the reason is structural: the outcome was never recorded as the action ran, only the activity around it.

Curious where people land:
- We can, by construction
- We can, but it is a manual effort each time

## The one-line position

DTA produces a **tested, evidenced, vendor-neutral specification for an agentic (and soon tokenised) consumer journey, before a bank builds it** — on a working proving-ground platform that already runs agents under C3 controls with the evidence recorded. We sit **upstream of, and independent of, every implementation vendor and RegTech monitor.** We do not compete with them; we produce the spec that feeds them.

## The market, in four modes

Every incumbent does one of four things. None sits in the intersection we occupy.

| Mode | Who (examples) | What they actually do | The limit |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Conduct consulting** | Big 4 (Deloitte, EY, KPMG, PwC), boutiques | Consumer Duty frameworks, assurance, board-report review, target operating models | Advisory deliverables — slides, gap analyses. They describe what good looks like; they don't hand you a working artefact that runs the journey |
| **Consumer / behavioural testing** | UserTesting, Fairer Finance, Irrational Agency, BIT | Comprehension testing, A/B and RCTs, usability on communications | The unit is a screen or a letter. They test static assets, not an agent acting for the customer |
| **Consumer Duty RegTech** | FinregE, ACA ComplianceAlpha, ZIGRAM, LogicGate | Post-launch outcomes monitoring, MI dashboards, comms surveillance, board tooling | Post-launch and MI-shaped — the "repackaged data" the FCA criticises. Watches after the build, not before |
| **Agentic-AI / sandboxing** | Sardine, Parloa, Salesforce; FCA Supercharged Sandbox | Agent guardrails, sandbox simulation of agent flows, drift detection, audit readiness | Frames the agent as an operational/compliance risk (fraud, AML, audit). Tests the agent's behaviour, not the customer's experience of it under a Consumer Duty lens |

## The intersection nobody occupies

The DTA offering is the overlap of all four: test the **consumer outcome** (mode 2) of an **agentic journey running under controls** (mode 4), **before launch** with evidence generated as you go (the inverse of mode 3), framed for the regulator's actual question (mode 1's domain), and forward-pointed at **tokenised value** (which none of them can answer and none can quickly acquire).

The problem is acknowledged, not invented: American Banker ("AI agents are going to test the limits of bank compliance") and vendor playbooks both flag that autonomous agent actions can damage a customer's outcome even as the agent "optimises." They answer it as an operational control problem. **Nobody answers it as a Consumer Duty outcome problem, with real (test) consumers, pre-launch.** That reframing is the move.

## Why it holds up — and where it doesn't

**Innovation: high** at the intersection. We name and occupy a gap the market has only described as a control problem.

**Consulting differentiation: medium-high.** The deliverable is a tested, vendor-ready specification, not a framework. The *narrative* is copyable in a slide; the moat is the working agentic-under-C3 platform plus the evidence-by-construction spine plus payments/tokenisation depth — which the testing agencies and Big 4 do not have. Play it as a wedge (land via the prototype, expand into transformation), not head-on against Big 4 scale.

**Product differentiation: high, correctly framed.** The proving ground *is* the product, and it works. "Non-production" is the design, not a gap: it runs with test consumers and produces a specification, it never goes live with real customers. That makes vendor-neutrality a selling point to the bank (no lock-in) and a non-threat to implementers (channel, not rival).

**Biggest risk:** a well-resourced incumbent copies the framing before the capture/evidence layer is real enough to be the moat. **Biggest accelerant:** tokenisation — the one axis the testing and RegTech incumbents have no answer to, and which plays to DTA's payments-architecture heritage.

# DTA Controlled-Evolution Website Design

**Status:** Revised design for review

**Date:** 20 June 2026

**Scope:** DTA corporate website and its relationship to the Clean-Room Demonstration

## Decision

DTA will lead with strategic advisory. Staffing and agentic squads will remain visible, independently purchasable services, but they will support the advisory position rather than define the firm.

The website will evolve rather than rebrand. It will retain the established ideas of Owned Autonomy, C3, the Clean-Room Demonstration, Proposition Blueprints, and the Glass Tube. It will organise those ideas around one mission:

> **DTA's mission is to help banks and fintechs build the future of banking without leaving any consumer behind.**

The mission joins two commitments. DTA will help banks prepare for agentic and tokenised banking, and it will advocate for consumer outcomes and digital inclusion throughout that change. The working platform proves DTA's advice. DTA does not present itself as a software platform, core vendor, production migration platform, or data-mesh vendor.

## Objectives

The update must:

1. Establish DTA as a senior strategic advisory firm within the first screen.
2. Show that DTA can turn advice into working, inspectable proof.
3. Keep staffing, specialist talent, and managed squads easy to find and buy.
4. Make the Clean-Room Demonstration a source of credibility, not the corporate category.
5. Use *A Place to Start* as the flagship example of human outcomes, operating economics, and reusable architecture.
6. Make Consumer Outcomes a strong, public DTA advocacy position rather than a narrow testing service.
7. Introduce a synthetic bank migration twin that challenges core-first procurement by proving the target architecture and migration touchpoints before vendor selection.
8. Preserve the website's claim discipline: demonstrated, simulated, illustrative, specified, and blueprint capabilities remain visibly distinct.

## Positioning

### Corporate category

**Strategic advisory for the future of banking, proven in a working model.**

### Core promise

> We combine independent strategy with executable proof, helping banks and fintechs prepare for agentic and tokenised banking before committing to platforms, migration programmes, or operating-model change.

### Future-bank thesis

DTA's thesis is that a core replacement alone does not create the bank required for agentic and tokenised banking. Future-ready banks will need governed Party, Product, Contract, Account, Authority, Consent, Transaction, and Evidence capabilities that survive changes in channels, engines, and core platforms.

Public copy should state this as DTA's argument, not an inevitable forecast:

> Over the next decade, banks will need governed data products, programmable money, accountable agents, and continuous evidence. A core replacement alone does not create that capability.

The public challenge is architectural, not a named-vendor attack:

> Replacing the core is not the same as transforming the bank.

### Governing idea

**Owned Autonomy** remains DTA's doctrine: agentic capability a bank owns rather than rents, accountable and sovereign.

### Proof method

**Evidence by construction** describes how DTA proves decisions and controls. It is a method and differentiator, not a promise that a regulator has accepted or certified an artefact.

### Flagship proof points

- **A Place to Start** shows the mission in human terms: help one person without a fixed address, conventional documents, or a smartphone gain access to an ordinary basic bank account, while preserving the bank's accountability and the applicant's control.
- **Synthetic Bank Migration Twin** shows the strategic challenge: model a plausible legacy bank with synthetic data, prove the target architecture and migration touchpoints, and produce an executable vendor-neutral specification before a bank buys or migrates a core.

## Audiences and buying moments

### Strategic advisory buyers

- Bank and fintech CEOs and business-unit leaders shaping growth or transformation.
- CTOs and CIOs choosing architecture, platforms, and migration paths.
- COOs and transformation leaders reducing operating friction and exception cost.
- CDOs and CISOs governing data, authority, evidence, and agent action.
- Product, Consumer Duty, consumer-outcomes, and financial-inclusion leaders designing new propositions.

Their buying moments include a new proposition, an agentic strategy, a platform decision, a failing transformation, a regulatory concern, or a need to reduce the cost of manual exceptions.

### Staffing and squad buyers

- Technology and transformation leaders who need senior specialists quickly.
- Product and engineering leaders who need a managed squad for a bounded outcome.
- Executives who need a fractional technology, architecture, payments, or transformation leader.

Staffing can begin as a standalone engagement. It should also provide a route from advice into delivery without implying that clients must buy a transformation programme.

Example engagements:

### 3. Talent and squads

DTA supplies senior individuals, fractional leaders, forward platform engineers, or managed agentic squads. This route supports delivery and remains independently purchasable.

The copy must avoid body-shop language. It should sell accountable capability and bounded outcomes, not CV volume or cheap capacity.

## Consumer Outcomes advocacy

### Role on the site

Rename **Customer Outcomes** to **Consumer Outcomes** throughout the corporate website. Consumer Outcomes will become a primary navigation item and a visible DTA position, not merely a description of a testing engagement.

The public stance is:

> Good consumer outcomes are designed into a proposition, not evidenced after the damage is done.

DTA will advocate for banks to begin with the people most likely to be excluded, test propositions before code and process harden, preserve human agency when agents act, and generate evidence while decisions are made.

### Principles DTA advocates

1. **Outcomes before implementation.** A bank should define the intended consumer outcome, risks, support model, and measures before choosing the platform or building the journey.
2. **Design from the excluded edge.** A proposition that works for someone without a fixed address, conventional documents, or a smartphone exposes assumptions that ordinary happy-path design conceals.
3. **Human agency when agents act.** Consumers should understand when an agent acts, what authority it has, how to intervene, and where to find human support.
4. **Evidence as responsibility.** Evidence by construction should help a bank learn and remain accountable. It is not a shortcut to compliance or a claim of regulatory approval.
5. **Lived experience over synthetic confidence.** DTA should test important propositions with relevant consumers and specialist organisations before recommending implementation.
6. **Inclusion with economic discipline.** Better access should reduce avoidable exceptions, repeated contacts, and manual rework rather than transfer hidden cost to charities or consumers.

### Page message

**Proposed headline**

> Banking should work for the people it is easiest to exclude.

**Proposed introduction**

> Consumer Duty should change what a bank designs, not merely how it reports. DTA helps banks and fintechs design, test, and evidence propositions around real consumer needs before delivery choices harden into code, process, and cost.

The page should lead with advocacy, then show how DTA helps. It must not read as a generic Consumer Duty compliance service.

### Page structure

1. **The position:** consumer outcomes begin at proposition design.
2. **The people hidden by the happy path:** exclusion, vulnerability, low digital confidence, and lack of safe access.
3. **A Place to Start:** one person, one outcome, and one concrete challenge to current banking assumptions.
4. **What changes when agents act:** comprehension, authority, intervention, and human support.
5. **What DTA asks banks to do:** define, test, evidence, learn, and change before launch.
6. **How DTA helps:** Consumer Outcomes advisory, proposition testing, a Clean-Room Proof, and a vendor-neutral working specification.
7. **The evidence boundary:** demonstrated, illustrative, and blueprint claims remain explicit.
8. **Call to action:** **Bring us a proposition that could exclude someone.**

- Move the canonical page from `/customer-outcomes/` to `/consumer-outcomes/`.

## Synthetic Bank Migration Twin

### Role on the site

The Synthetic Bank Migration Twin is both a strategic-advisory proposition and a future Clean-Room Demonstration. It challenges the assumption that choosing a newer core defines the target bank. DTA will define the future architecture first, prove the migration shape with synthetic data, and use the result to inform procurement, migration, and operating-model decisions.

It is not a production migration platform and does not process live customer data.

### Public message

**Headline**

> Before you buy another core, prove the bank you actually need.

**Thesis**

> Replacing the core is not the same as transforming the bank. A modern product or ledger engine can be part of the answer, but the future bank also needs governed products, enforceable authority, purpose-bound data, programmable value, accountable agents, and evidence that survives another platform change.

**Advisory promise**

> DTA creates an independent target architecture, a synthetic migration proof, and a vendor-neutral specification before implementation choices harden.

### Demonstration scope

The proposition should follow one synthetic customer cohort through:

1. A plausible legacy dataset containing parties, products, contracts, accounts, balances, transactions, mandates, authorities, support needs, and control totals.
2. Mapping from legacy records into governed target data products.
3. Opening-state and transaction-history reconciliation.
4. Simulated payment, treasury, finance, identity, reporting, and sort-code migration touchpoints, including a labelled BPRS interaction.
5. Cohort-level source-of-truth switching in the model.
6. A payment sent using legacy details, redirected once, received once, and reconciled.
7. Preservation of terms, authority, consent, and safe-servicing requirements.
8. A rollback demonstration.
9. A migration decision record, exception report, and vendor-neutral specification.

### Real, synthetic, and specified boundary

**Demonstrated** means the transformation, policy, reconciliation, routing, and evidence mechanism executes in the prototype.

**Simulated** means the legacy-bank dataset, mainframe extracts, customer calls, scheme responses, BPRS interaction, payment and treasury services, and production operating events are generated test fixtures.

**Specified** means the output describes production scale, live cutover, operational staffing, scheme certification, security controls, and migration obligations without claiming DTA has executed them in this demonstration.

Each migration step and output must carry one of these labels. Do not use *digital twin* without the qualifier *synthetic* until the Phase 9 feedback and reconciliation behaviour is demonstrated.

### Core and shared-services model

Use the public formulation:

> Parallel-run the core, not the whole bank. Share execution, duplicate calculation, and reconcile every outcome before authority moves.

The specification should show one execution with two representations:

- A payment, treasury action, card event, or colleague identity occurs operationally once.
- Legacy and target models may calculate, book, and compare the result.
- The model must never imply duplicate scheme submission, duplicate settlement, or a live dual-write design.

### Outputs

- Independent target-architecture decision record.
- Legacy-to-target semantic map.
- Source-of-truth matrix by cohort, domain, capability, and effective date.
- Reconciliation results for money, contracts, authority, service, and consumer outcomes.
- Migration exceptions and unresolved decisions.
- Synthetic touchpoint catalogue.
- Vendor-neutral procurement and implementation specification.

### Private competitor treatment

Do not name Thought Machine, Pismo, or other vendors in homepage attack copy. Maintain private procurement and competitor material that asks whether each product supports the target architecture. Public copy should challenge core-first decision-making, not disparage individual suppliers.

## Language and claim rules

### Use

- Strategic advisory.
- Independent target architecture.
- Working proving ground.
- Interactive demonstration.
- Blueprint.
- Evidence by construction.
- Vendor-neutral working specification.
- Designed for assisted web, telephone, and branch delivery.
- The supporter supplies bounded evidence; the bank makes the CDD decision.

### Do not use without new substantiation

- The only or the first.
- Regulator-accepted or regulator-approved evidence.
- Compliance by construction.
- Signed evidence package.
- Live proposition or live engine.
- Three-minute onboarding.
- Charity-attested identity.
- Anchor Identity as the public proposition name.
- Anchor Edition or any second-class product label.
- Zero PII unless the full journey and every projection have been verified.
- The core becomes irrelevant.
- All banks must adopt this architecture.
- No consumer will accept current servicing models.
- A named vendor is last-generation or obsolete.
- Digital twin without *synthetic* before the Phase 9 feedback and reconciliation loop is demonstrated.
- Production migration, live-customer migration, scheme certification, or operational-scale claims.

Every new material statement must pass through `docs/claim-register.md`. Demonstration claims must also match the prototype's marketing claim audit.

## Acceptance criteria

The design is complete when:

- Strategic advisory leads the hero, navigation, and offer order.
- The selected mission appears directly below the hero and on the About page.
- The homepage explains agentic and tokenised banking without presenting a forecast as certainty.
- Staffing remains visible from the homepage and primary navigation.
- Consumer Outcomes appears in primary navigation and leads with a clear advocacy position.
- The Consumer Outcomes page distinguishes public advocacy from the advisory services that support it.
- Visitors can understand and buy either route independently.
- The demonstration supports the advisory claim without making DTA look like a platform vendor.
- A Place to Start leads with one person's outcome and carries an honest status label.
- The Synthetic Bank Migration Twin distinguishes demonstrated mechanisms, simulated inputs, specified production requirements, and blueprint scope.
- Public copy challenges core-first procurement without attacking named vendors.
- Every public capability statement agrees with the claim register.
- The site distinguishes demonstrated, illustrative, and blueprint content.
- Mobile navigation and page hierarchy remain accessible.
- Each primary page has one clear action tied to the visitor's intent.

## Out of scope

- A visual rebrand.
- Turning the Clean-Room Demonstration into a licensed software product.
- Building a production migration platform or connecting live customer data.

- Publishing internal schemas, policy logic, or private architecture codenames.
